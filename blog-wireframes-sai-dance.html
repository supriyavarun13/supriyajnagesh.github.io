Why I Started a Salesforce Project With Wireframes, Not With Salesforce

A walkthrough of the Sai Dance Academy enrollment build — and what it says about how I approach a project before touching Setup. The wireframes below are live and interactive.

Most Salesforce projects I've seen go wrong don't fail because of a bad Flow or a broken trigger. They fail earlier than that — at the moment someone opens Setup and starts building before anyone has agreed on what the experience should actually feel like for the person on the other end.

So when I started building a public enrollment and payment system for a dance academy, I didn't open Salesforce first. I opened a blank canvas and drew three wireframes. This article walks through those wireframes and the reasoning behind them — because the wireframes are where the admin work really begins, long before a single field gets created.

If you're hiring, this is the part of my thinking I'd want you to see.

The project in one paragraph

The academy needed a way for parents and adult students to enroll in dance classes online and pay in the same sitting. The requirements were ordinary on the surface: a public homepage, an enrollment form, online payment, and an automatic confirmation. Underneath, it's a fairly complete Salesforce build — a public-facing Experience Cloud site backed by Lightning Web Components, Apex handling the server-side logic, a Stripe integration for payments, webhooks writing back to Salesforce, and a Flow firing the confirmation email. The wireframes were how I made sure all of that machinery stayed in service of a clean experience.

Wireframe 1 — The homepage

The homepage wireframe looks deceptively simple: a nav bar, a hero with two calls to action, a row of featured classes, an events-and-gallery split, a footer, and a persistent AI agent bubble in the bottom-right corner.

What matters here isn't the layout — it's the decisions baked into it.

Decision 01
Two hero CTAs — "View Classes" and "Enroll Now"
The visitor who's still browsing needs a different path than the one who already knows what they want. A single CTA serves neither well. Two CTAs cost nothing in the wireframe and save a bounce in production.
Decision 02
Featured classes row implies a data model decision
The featured-classes row isn't decoration — it implies a Dance_Class__c object with a boolean flag for what's promoted. Drawing the homepage forced me to define that field before I built the object, not discover it was missing halfway through.
Decision 03
Persistent AI agent bubble
Deliberately always visible so a parent with a quick question ("Is there a beginner class for a 7-year-old?") never has to hunt for help. This also meant defining the agent's knowledge source and guardrails before deployment — decisions that appear in the Agentforce post.

Wireframe 2 — The enrollment-to-payment flow

This is the heart of the build, and I wireframed it as three connected screens rather than one — because the user is really moving through three distinct moments: giving their details, paying, and being reassured it worked. A three-step progress indicator runs across all of them so the user always knows where they are and how much is left.

Step 1 — The enrollment form

First name, last name, email, phone, a class-selection dropdown, an age-group dropdown, and a GDPR consent checkbox — with a live order summary sitting alongside the form. This screen is a Lightning Web Component. When the user submits, the LWC calls Apex, Apex creates a Stripe Checkout session, and the user is redirected to pay.

🧠 Why the order summary is on the same screen

People abandon forms when they can't see what they're committing to before they hand over a card. The order summary on the enrollment screen is a conversion decision, not a design flourish. It reduces abandonment by giving the visitor certainty before they leave for Stripe.

✅ GDPR by design, not by afterthought

The consent checkbox is in the wireframe — not bolted on after a legal review flagged the form. Building compliance into the wireframe is the difference between a form that's launch-ready and one that gets sent back. This is a habit, not a one-off.

Step 2 — Stripe hosted checkout

This screen is fully hosted by Stripe. I deliberately kept payment off our own infrastructure. Letting Stripe own the card-entry screen means the academy never touches raw card data — which collapses the PCI compliance burden dramatically. That's a security and risk decision expressed as a wireframe choice. When payment succeeds, a Stripe webhook fires back to Salesforce Apex.

Step 3 — The confirmation page

A success checkmark, the enrollment reference number, the class details, and two CTAs: "View all classes" and "Back to home." By this point the webhook has updated Salesforce and a Flow has already sent the confirmation email. The on-screen confirmation and the emailed one are doing different jobs — one gives immediate closure, the other gives a permanent record. The wireframe is where I made sure both existed.

Why this matters for an admin role specifically

It would have been faster to skip the wireframes and start clicking. But the wireframes are where I made the decisions that are expensive to change later: which objects exist, where compliance lives, how the integration hands control back and forth between Salesforce and Stripe, and where automation fires.

By the time I opened Setup, the build was almost mechanical. The LWC knew what fields it needed because the form was already drawn. The Apex knew its job because the redirect-and-webhook handshake was already mapped. The Flow knew its trigger because the confirmation step was already defined. The architecture diagram and the user experience were the same document.

💡 The habit this represents

Treating the admin's job as designing a system end to end — data model, automation, integration, and compliance — in service of an experience a real person can move through without friction. The platform is the tool. The wireframe is where the thinking happens.
